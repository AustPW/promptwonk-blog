Here's a prompt someone actually sent to ChatGPT last week:

```
help me with my presentation
```

They got a generic five-slide outline about "tips for effective presentations." It wasn't wrong. It was useless.

The person wasn't stupid. They were a marketing director at a SaaS company. They knew what they wanted — a competitive analysis deck for their board meeting. But they treated the AI like a mind reader instead of a tool.

This is the default behavior for about 90% of people using AI tools. And it's fixable.

## The Three Mistakes Everyone Makes

### Mistake 1: Being Vague When You Need to Be Specific

The biggest gap between good and bad prompts isn't length — it's specificity. Vague prompts produce vague output.

```
Vague:  "Write a blog post about React."
Better: "Write an 800-word blog post explaining React Server Components
         for developers who know React but haven't used the App Router.
         Focus on the mental model shift, not the API details.
         Include one practical example."
```

The vague version could produce literally anything about React. The specific version constrains the model to produce something useful.

**The fix:** Before writing a prompt, answer three questions:
1. What specifically do I want? (Not "a blog post" — what *kind* of blog post?)
2. Who is this for? (Their knowledge level and needs)
3. What does "good" look like? (Length, format, tone, depth)

### Mistake 2: Dumping Context Instead of Structuring It

People who've learned that "more context is better" often swing too far the other direction. They paste walls of text and hope the model figures out what matters.

```
Bad context dump:
"Here's our company info [3 pages of text]. We need marketing copy.
Our competitors are [2 pages]. Here's our product spec [5 pages].
Write something."

Structured context:
"You're writing landing page copy for Acme Analytics.

Key facts:
- Product: Real-time dashboard for e-commerce metrics
- Target user: E-commerce managers at mid-size brands ($5M-$50M revenue)
- Main differentiator: Connects all platforms (Shopify, Amazon, WooCommerce) in one view
- Tone: Professional but not corporate, data-driven

Write a hero section (headline + subheadline + 2 bullet points).
Keep it under 75 words total."
```

**The fix:** Distill your context into bullet points before including it. If you can't summarize a piece of context in one line, the model probably can't use it effectively either.

### Mistake 3: Accepting the First Output

This is the subtlest mistake and the most common. Most people send a prompt, read the response, and either use it or start over with a completely different prompt.

The problem: the first response is almost never the best one. It's a starting point.

Good prompt engineers treat the first output as a draft. They read it critically and then give specific feedback:

```
Round 1: "Write an email declining a vendor's proposal."
[gets a generic polite rejection]

Round 2: "Good structure, but:
- Make the tone warmer — we want to work with them in the future
- Add a specific reason (budget constraints for Q2)
- Suggest reconnecting in Q3
- Keep it under 150 words"
```

**The fix:** Commit to at least two rounds on anything that matters. The first round gets the shape right. The second round makes it actually good.

## The Mental Model Shift

Here's the core insight most people miss: **you are not asking a question. You are writing a specification.**

When you google something, you write a query. When you prompt an AI, you're writing a brief — like you would for a designer, a copywriter, or a contractor.

Think about it: you'd never hire a freelance writer and say "write me something about marketing." You'd give them a brief with the topic, audience, length, tone, examples of what you like, and what you definitely don't want.

Prompting is the same thing. The AI is your contractor. Your prompt is the brief.

## A Framework for Better Prompts

You don't need a complex template. Just hit these five elements:

1. **Role** — Who should the AI be? (optional but powerful)
2. **Task** — What exactly do you want?
3. **Context** — What does the AI need to know?
4. **Format** — How should the output look?
5. **Constraints** — What should it avoid?

Here's how that looks in practice:

```
[Role] You're a data analyst explaining findings to a non-technical CEO.

[Task] Summarize these Q4 sales results.

[Context]
- Revenue: $2.3M (up 15% QoQ)
- New customers: 142 (down 8% QoQ)
- Churn: 3.2% (stable)
- Top product: Enterprise plan (68% of revenue)

[Format] 3-4 sentences, then 2 bullet points for recommended actions.

[Constraints] No jargon. No charts. Focus on what the CEO should do,
not what happened.
```

This takes 30 seconds longer to write than "summarize these sales results." The output is 10x more useful.

## Common Patterns That Kill Prompt Quality

**Over-politeness:** "Could you please kindly help me with writing a nice email?" Just say "Write an email that..."

**Hedge words:** "Maybe you could try to give me something like a summary?" These signal uncertainty and the model mirrors it.

**Compound requests without structure:** "Write a blog post and also create social media posts and an email subject line and maybe a meta description." Split these into separate requests or explicitly structure them.

**Assuming context from previous conversations:** Each prompt should contain everything the model needs. Don't assume it "remembers" the nuance from five messages ago — its attention degrades over long conversations.

## The Skill That Pays Off

Prompt engineering isn't a separate skill from your actual job. It's a multiplier on everything you already do.

A marketer who writes specific prompts produces better first drafts in half the time. A developer who structures their debugging prompts fixes issues faster. A manager who writes clear AI briefs gets usable outputs instead of generic templates.

The gap between "uses AI" and "uses AI well" is mostly about the 30 seconds of thought before you type. That's the habit to build.

Start with your next prompt. Before you hit enter, re-read it and ask: "If I sent this brief to a smart intern who knows nothing about my project, would they know exactly what to produce?"

If the answer is no, keep editing.
